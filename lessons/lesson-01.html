Lesson 1: Alphabet, Pronunciation & Greetings

Master the Indonesian alphabet, pronunciation rules, and essential greetings for daily communication.

The Indonesian Alphabet

Indonesian uses the Latin alphabet (A-Z) just like English, but with some important pronunciation differences. The good news is that Indonesian is phonetic - words are pronounced exactly as they're spelled!

Key Pronunciation Rules

  • C is always pronounced like 'ch' in "chair"
  • E has two sounds: like 'e' in "bed" OR like 'a' in "ago"
  • Q is pronounced 'k' (rare, used for foreign words)
  • R is rolled or tapped like in Spanish
  • V is pronounced 'f' (used for foreign words)

Essential Greetings

Greetings in Indonesia often depend on the time of day. Here are the most important ones:

Selamat pagi
Good morning (from sunrise until ~11 am)
suh-lah-mat pah-gee
Selamat siang
Good day (from ~11 am until ~3 pm)
suh-lah-mat see-ahng
Selamat sore
Good afternoon/evening (from ~3 pm until sunset)
suh-lah-mat sor-ay
Selamat malam
Good night (after sunset)
suh-lah-mat mah-lahm
Apa kabar?
How are you?
ah-pah kah-bar
Kabar baik
I'm well (Literally: "good news")
kah-bar bah-eek

Basic Introductions

Learn how to introduce yourself and ask for someone's name:

Cultural Note

The Spirit of Indonesian Surf Culture

In Indonesia, a smile goes a long way. People are generally very friendly and polite. When greeting someone, especially someone older, a slight nod of the head is a sign of respect.

Don't be surprised if people you've just met ask you personal questions like "Are you married?" or "Where are you going?"—it's a common way to be friendly and make conversation, not to be nosy!

In surf areas like Bali, using the local greeting "Om Swastiastu" shows great respect for the local culture. Places like Nias Point (pictured above) have been legendary surf destinations since the 1980s, where surfers discovered perfect waves and warm hospitality.
