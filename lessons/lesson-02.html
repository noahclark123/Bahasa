Lesson 2: Pronouns & Simple Questions

Learn personal pronouns and how to ask basic questions in Indonesian.

Subject Pronouns

Pronouns in Indonesian are simple because they don't change based on their position in a sentence. Here are the most important ones:

Saya
I, me, my
sah-yah
Can be used in formal and informal situations
Anda
You (formal, polite)
an-dah
Use with strangers, elders, or in business
Kamu
You (informal)
kah-moo
Use with friends, family, people your age or younger
Dia
He, she, him, her
dee-ah
There is no gender in Indonesian pronouns!
Kita
We, us (inclusive)
kee-tah
Includes the person you're talking to
Kami
We, us (exclusive)
kah-mee
Does NOT include the person you're talking to
Mereka
They, them
meh-reh-kah
Used for multiple people

Important: Kita vs Kami

The distinction between kita and kami is important. If you and a friend are talking to a third person and say, "Kita pergi ke toko" (We are going to the store), it means all three of you are going. If you say, "Kami pergi ke toko," it means only you and your friend are going, not the person you're speaking to.

Forming Simple "Yes/No" Questions

The easiest way to form a yes/no question is to take a statement and say it with a rising intonation at the end.

Anda sehat.
You are healthy. (Statement)
an-dah suh-hat
Anda sehat?
Are you healthy? (Question)
an-dah suh-hat? (with rising intonation)

Using -kah Particle

Another common way is to add the particle -kah to the word you are questioning, which is often the first word. This is slightly more formal.

Apakah Anda sehat?
Are you healthy?
ah-pah-kah an-dah suh-hat

"Apa" is a question word, and adding -kah makes it clear it's a yes/no question.

Asking "What?" and "Where?" Questions

What Questions

Apa?
What?
ah-pah
Apa ini?
What is this?
ah-pah ee-nee
Ini apa?
What is this? (more common in speech)
ee-nee ah-pah
Ini buku.
This is a book. (Response)
ee-nee boo-koo

Where Questions

Di mana?
Where?
dee mah-nah
Di mana toilet?
Where is the toilet?
dee mah-nah toy-let
Toilet di sana.
The toilet is over there. (Response)
toy-let dee sah-nah

Asking for Directions & Surf Info

Here are some useful phrases for travelers and surfers:

Di mana pantai [Beach Name]?
Where is [Beach Name] beach?
dee mah-nah pan-thai [Beach Name]
Belok kiri / Belok kanan / Lurus saja
Turn left / Turn right / Go straight
buh-lok kee-ree / buh-lok kah-nan / loo-roos sah-jah
Aman untuk berselancar?
Is it safe to surf?
ah-mahn oon-took bur-suh-lan-char
Di mana saya bisa sewa papan selancar?
Where can I rent a surfboard?
dee mah-nah sah-yah bee-sah seh-wah pah-pahn suh-lan-char
Berapa harga untuk satu jam?
How much for one hour?
buh-rah-pah har-gah oon-took sah-too jahm

Cultural Note

Jam Karet (Rubber Time)

Have you ever heard of jam karet, or "rubber time"? It's a well-known concept in Indonesia that reflects a more relaxed and flexible approach to time and punctuality. While it's not an excuse to be late for important business meetings, it's common for social gatherings to start a bit later than scheduled. It's part of the laid-back culture.

So if you're meeting a friend and they're 15-30 minutes late, don't worry—it's likely just a case of jam karet! For surfing, however, be mindful of the tides (pasang surut)—nature doesn't operate on rubber time!
