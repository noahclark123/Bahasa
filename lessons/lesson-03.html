Lesson 3: Numbers, Days & Time

Learn to count, tell time, and discuss days of the week in Indonesian.

Numbers 1-100

Numbers in Indonesian follow a very logical pattern. Learn 1-10, and the rest becomes much easier.

Number Patterns

For 11-19: You add the word belas after the number (1-9). Satu becomes se-.

  • 11 - sebelas (se + belas)
  • 12 - dua belas
  • 13 - tiga belas
  • ...and so on up to 19 (sembilan belas)

For the tens (20, 30, etc.): You add the word puluh.

  • 20 - dua puluh
  • 21 - dua puluh satu
  • 30 - tiga puluh
  • ...and so on

100: seratus (se + ratus)

Asking "How much/many?"

The question word Berapa? is incredibly useful, especially when shopping.

Berapa?
How much / How many?
buh-rah-pah
Berapa harganya?
How much is the price? (Very common)
buh-rah-pah har-gah-nyah

Telling Time

Learn how to ask for and tell the time in Indonesian:

Jam berapa sekarang?
What time is it now?
jahm buh-rah-pah suh-kah-rahng

Jam means "hour". Berapa means "how many". So, "How many hours?"

Stating the Time

To state the time, you say Jam + the hour.

Jam satu.
It's 1 o'clock
jahm sah-too
Jam lima.
It's 5 o'clock
jahm lee-mah

Minutes Past the Hour

For minutes past the hour, use lewat.

Jam tiga lewat sepuluh.
3:10 (Hour three pass ten)
jahm tee-gah leh-what suh-poo-looh
Jam tujuh lewat dua puluh lima.
7:25 (Hour seven pass twenty-five)
jahm too-jooh leh-what doo-ah poo-looh lee-mah

Practical Applications for Travel

Using numbers is essential for daily transactions:

Sewa motor, dua jam.
Rent a motorbike, two hours
seh-wah mo-tor, doo-ah jahm
Kamar ini, tiga malam.
This room, for three nights
kah-mar ee-nee, tee-gah mah-lahm
Pasang jam berapa?
What time is high tide?
pah-sahng jahm buh-rah-pah
Surut jam berapa?
What time is low tide?
soo-root jahm buh-rah-pah

Cultural Note

Bargaining (Tawar-menawar)

Bargaining, or tawar-menawar, is a common and expected practice in traditional markets (pasar), with street vendors, and even for services like renting a scooter or surfboard in Indonesia. It's a friendly and social interaction, not an argument.

The first price offered is often inflated. As a foreigner, you might be given a higher price, but a smile and a polite "Bisa kurang?" (Can it be less?) can open up a negotiation. Never start with a ridiculously low offer, and always keep the interaction light and friendly. It's a fun way to practice your numbers!
