Adjective Placement

This is a key difference from English! In Indonesian, the adjective always comes AFTER the noun it describes.

Pattern: Noun + Adjective

Mobil besar
A big car (Literally: "Car big")
mo-beel buh-sar
Kopi panas
Hot coffee (Literally: "Coffee hot")
ko-pee pah-nahs
Wanita cantik
A beautiful woman (Literally: "Woman beautiful")
wah-nee-tah chan-tik
Ombak besar
Big waves (Literally: "Waves big")
ohm-bak buh-sar
Pantai sepi
Quiet beach (Literally: "Beach quiet")
pan-thai suh-pee

Possession

Showing possession is simple. You just put the pronoun after the noun. It's the same structure as with adjectives (Noun + Owner).

Rumah saya
My house (Literally: "House I")
roo-mah sah-yah
Nama Anda
Your name (Literally: "Name you")
nah-mah an-dah
Buku dia
His/Her book (Literally: "Book he/she")
boo-koo dee-ah

Describing Conditions & Experiences

Use adjectives to share your experiences:

Ombaknya sangat besar!
The waves are very big! (sangat = very)
ohm-bak-nyah sah-ngat buh-sar
Warung ini murah dan enak.
This eatery is cheap and delicious.
wah-roong ee-nee moo-rah dahn eh-nak
Orang di sini sangat baik.
The people here are very kind.
or-ahng dee see-nee sah-ngat bah-eek
Hati-hati, arusnya kuat.
Be careful, the current is strong.
hah-tee hah-tee, ah-roos-nyah koo-at

Cultural Note

Respectful Address

The words for mother (Ibu) and father (Bapak) are also very important terms of respect. You use Bapak (or the shortened Pak) to respectfully address any man who is older than you or in a position of authority (e.g., a teacher, a boss, a government official).

Similarly, you use Ibu (or Bu) to address any older or respected woman. Using "Pak" or "Bu" before someone's name (e.g., Pak Joko, Bu Siti) is the standard polite way to address them.

This politeness extends to the surf lineup; a smile, a nod, and giving way to local surfers are key to a good session.
